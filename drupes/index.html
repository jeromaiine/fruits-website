Drupes

What is a Drupe?

A drupe is a type of fruit characterized by having three distinct layers: a thin outer skin (exocarp), a fleshy middle layer (mesocarp), and a hard, stony inner layer (endocarp) that surrounds a single seed. Common examples include fruits like peaches, cherries, and olives. The defining feature of a drupe is this कठोर “pit” or “stone”, which protects the seed inside. 5 Types of Drupes

Peach

A peach is a soft, fuzzy-skinned stone fruit (meaning it has a hard pit inside) that grows on deciduous trees.

Scientific name:
Prunus persica
Taste:
Sweet and juicy with a slight tang; very fragrant and floral when ripe

Cherry

A cherry is a small, round stone fruit that comes in sweet and sour varieties, usually red or dark purple.

Scientific name:
Prunus avium (sweet cherry) / Prunus cerasus (sour cherry)
Taste:
Sweet cherries: mild, sugary, and juicy
Sour cherries: tart and slightly acidic

Plums

A plum is a smooth-skinned stone fruit that comes in many colors (purple, red, yellow) and sizes.

Scientific name:
Prunus domestica (European plum) / Prunus salicina (Japanese plum)
Taste:
Ranges from sweet to tart; juicy flesh with a slightly tangy skin.

Apricots

An apricot is a small, orange-colored stone fruit related to peaches, often eaten fresh or dried.

Scientific name:
Prunus armeniaca
Taste:
Sweet with a mild tartness; less juicy than peaches, with a slightly creamy texture.

Olives

A olive is a small fruit from the olive tree, commonly processed and used for oil or eaten cured.

Scientific name:
Olea europaea
Taste:
Naturally very bitter when raw; after curing, it becomes savory, salty, and sometimes slightly tangy or rich.

Nutritional Benefits

Drupes—fruits like peaches, cherries, plums, and olives—offer a range of nutritional benefits thanks to their rich vitamin, mineral, and antioxidant content. They are typically high in vitamin C and vitamin A, which support immune health and skin function, while also providing dietary fiber that aids digestion. Many drupes contain beneficial plant compounds such as polyphenols, which help reduce inflammation and oxidative stress. Some, like olives and avocados, are also good sources of healthy monounsaturated fats that support heart health.

High in antioxidants
Drupes are loaded with antioxidants such as polyphenols and carotenoids. For example, cherries and plums contain compounds that help reduce inflammation and may lower the risk of chronic diseases.
Rich in vitamins
Most drupes are high in vitamin C, which supports immune function and skin health, and vitamin A (especially in orange/yellow fruits like peaches and mangoes), which is important for vision and cell growth.
Hydration and low calorie density
Many drupes (like peaches and plums) have high water content, making them refreshing, hydrating, and relatively low in calories.

Interesting Drupe Facts

  • Almonds and coconuts are also technically drupes, even though they don’t look like typical fruits
  • The pits of certain drupes can release toxic compounds if broken or eaten
  • The word “drupe” comes from the Greek word druppa, meaning overripe olive
